# Practice Questions — AI Supply Chain Practitioner

A 16-item concept-check bank (2 per domain) for formative use by the tutor. These are **practice** items for understanding — not the certification exam. Each item lists the answer and a short rationale.

> Tutor usage: present one stem at a time, invite a guess, give a hint before confirming, and explain the rationale warmly regardless of whether the learner is right.

## Domain 1 — Foundations of AI in Supply Chain

**Q1.** What most distinguishes an AI/ML demand model from a traditional rules-based one?
A. It never needs data  B. It learns patterns from historical data rather than fixed if-then rules  C. It removes the need for any human oversight  D. It only works for retail
**Answer: B.** ML generalizes patterns from data; rules-based systems apply pre-set logic and don't learn.

**Q2.** Which is the best first question before launching any AI supply chain project?
A. Which vendor has the flashiest demo  B. What decision will this improve, and is the data available  C. How many GPUs we can buy  D. Whether competitors use AI
**Answer: B.** Value comes from improving a specific decision with adequate data — not from the technology itself.

## Domain 2 — Demand Forecasting & Planning with AI

**Q3.** MAPE measures:
A. Total revenue  B. Average percentage error between forecast and actual demand  C. Supplier lead time  D. Warehouse capacity
**Answer: B.** MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) summarizes forecast accuracy as an average percent error.

**Q4.** A forecast that is consistently *below* actual demand exhibits:
A. Zero error  B. Negative bias (under-forecasting)  C. High service level  D. Seasonality
**Answer: B.** Persistent one-directional error is bias; under-forecasting risks stockouts.

## Domain 3 — Inventory Optimization

**Q5.** Holding more safety stock primarily:
A. Lowers service level  B. Raises holding cost but reduces stockout risk  C. Eliminates lead time  D. Has no trade-offs
**Answer: B.** Safety stock buys protection against variability at the cost of carrying inventory.

**Q6.** An AI model raises the reorder point for an SKU. The most likely driver is:
A. Longer or more variable lead time / demand  B. The warehouse is empty  C. The supplier lowered prices  D. Nothing — reorder points are fixed
**Answer: A.** Reorder points rise when demand or lead-time variability increases.

## Domain 4 — Logistics, Routing & Transportation

**Q7.** In route optimization, "constraints" are:
A. Marketing goals  B. Real limits like vehicle capacity, time windows, and driver hours  C. The number of SKUs  D. Forecast error
**Answer: B.** Optimizers minimize cost/distance subject to operational constraints.

**Q8.** An AI routing tool proposes a plan that violates delivery time windows. You should:
A. Deploy it anyway  B. Treat it as a flag — check the constraints/data before acting  C. Delete all routing tools  D. Ignore the time windows
**Answer: B.** Outputs that break known constraints signal a data or configuration issue; verify, don't blindly trust.

## Domain 5 — Procurement & Supplier Intelligence

**Q9.** A supplier risk score is most useful when it:
A. Is a single static number set once  B. Combines multiple signals (financial, delivery, geo, compliance) and updates  C. Only reflects price  D. Is hidden from buyers
**Answer: B.** Good risk scoring is multi-factor and refreshed as conditions change.

**Q10.** "Spend analytics" with AI mainly helps procurement by:
A. Writing contracts automatically  B. Surfacing patterns like maverick spend and consolidation opportunities  C. Replacing all suppliers  D. Setting customer prices
**Answer: B.** It reveals where and how money is spent to inform sourcing decisions.

## Domain 6 — Warehouse Automation & Robotics

**Q11.** Computer vision in a warehouse is commonly used for:
A. Negotiating with suppliers  B. Scanning, sorting, and detecting damage or misplacement  C. Forecasting GDP  D. Writing emails
**Answer: B.** Vision systems support identification, sortation, and quality checks.

**Q12.** Before automating a warehouse process with AI/robotics, the soundest step is:
A. Automate the most complex task first  B. Pilot on a bounded, high-volume task and measure impact  C. Remove all staff immediately  D. Skip measurement
**Answer: B.** Start bounded, prove value and safety, then scale.

## Domain 7 — Risk, Resilience & End-to-End Visibility

**Q13.** A "control tower" provides:
A. Real-time end-to-end visibility to detect and respond to disruptions  B. A place to store pallets  C. A pricing engine  D. A payroll system
**Answer: A.** Control towers centralize visibility and exception management across the network.

**Q14.** A "digital twin" of a supply chain is:
A. A backup data center  B. A virtual model used to simulate and test scenarios before acting  C. A duplicate supplier  D. A type of contract
**Answer: B.** Digital twins let you test "what-if" scenarios safely in a virtual replica.

## Domain 8 — Responsible AI, Governance & Implementation

**Q15.** Why keep a human in the loop on high-impact AI supply chain decisions?
A. To slow everything down  B. To catch errors/bias and own accountability for the decision  C. Because AI is illegal  D. It isn't necessary
**Answer: B.** Human oversight manages error/bias and preserves accountability.

**Q16.** When building the business case for an AI initiative, the strongest evidence is:
A. A competitor's press release  B. A quantified link to a decision and its expected cost/service impact  C. The model's parameter count  D. The vendor's logo
**Answer: B.** Stakeholders fund measurable improvements to real decisions and outcomes.
